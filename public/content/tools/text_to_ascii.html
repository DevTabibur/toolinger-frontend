What is ASCII?

The American Standard Code for Information Interchange is known as ASCII. ASCII is a 7-bit character encoding standard, meaning that it defines 128 unique characters. These are the letters (both capital and small), numbers, punctuation, and control characters.

For example, A in ASCII decimal is 65, and a is 97. This simple plan allowed the first computers and teletypes to share text in a common way between machines.

Moreover, ASCII is not just historical—it is still the foundation of text encoding for modern systems. In fact, all characters in the first 128 Unicode values are the same as in the original ASCII standard.

ASCII vs. Extended ASCII vs. Unicode

It is easy to confuse ASCII with other encoding standards:

  • ASCII (Standard 7-bit): 128 characters, values 0–127, including letters, numbers, symbols, and control codes.

  • Extended ASCII (8-bit): Adds 128 more characters (256 total), including accented letters and other symbols.

  • Unicode: A large system that encodes over 143,000 characters from many languages and scripts, including emojis.

So, while modern systems use Unicode, ASCII remains the most compatible and simplest method of representing text, especially in programming, debugging, and data conversion uses.

In addition, control characters (Dec 0–31) provide basic purposes in data streams, like CR (Carriage Return) or LF (Line Feed). They cannot be seen in text but are vital in programming, file format, and communication protocols.

Becoming Familiar with Control Characters

Control characters are not enigmatic, but they play an important role:

  • CR (Carriage Return, 13): Moves the cursor to the beginning of the line.

  • LF (Line Feed, 10): Moves the cursor one line down.

  • BEL (Bell, 7): Makes a bell sound in some systems.

As such, knowledge of these codes can help developers debug data, diagnose encoding issues with text, or even create complex ASCII art that relies on spacing and line breaks.
